Why We Need Semi-Supervised Learning (SSL)?

Machine learning is a powerful tool for big, complex data, and it generally falls into two categories: supervised and unsupervised learning. These two categories seem to fit all types of problems, so why we need semi-supervised learning (SSL)? Before introducing SSL, we first review supervised and unsupervised learning.

Supervised learning algorithms are trained on labeled data, meaning that both input and output data are provided to the model for learning. We denote this collection of labeled data points \(D_L\) = \((x_i, y_i), \,i = 1, , N\). Usually we assume there is an underlying relationship between Y and X as \(Y = f(X) + \epsilon\). The algorithms try to capture this relationship \(f\) and provide an estimate \(\hat{f}\). The aim of the training step is to minimize \[L = \frac{1}{N}\sum^N_{i=1}l(\hat{f}(x_i),\,y_i), \text{ where } l \text{ is the loss function.}\] The estimated relationship \(\hat{f}\) is then used to predict the output for new, unseen data. (survey paper)

Unsupervised learning algorithms, on the other hand, deal with unlabeled data and primarily focus on identifying patterns or clusters within the data. Here we only have a set of features \(D_U\) = \(x_i, \,i = 1, , N\). Since we dont have an associated label, the algorithm aims to generate artificial labels to summarize the interesting pattern in the data, and the objective function would focus on these generated labels. If we have \(C = \{C_1, ..., C_k\}\) partitioning for k clusters, we aim to minimize \[L = \sum^k_{i=1}\sum_{x_m,\,x_n \in C_i}d(x_m, \,x_n), \text{ where } d \text{ is the distance matric.}\] (survey and isl)

In many real-world scenarios, however, data often come in a mix of labeled and unlabeled forms. In other words, we have sets \(D_L\) and \(D_U\) at the same time, and usually the number of data in \(D_U\) is much larger. For example, in a medical study, many biopsy samples may be collected, but only a fraction are annotated by pathologists within a given period. (bladder cancer)

In a financial context, an institution may know the credit scores of certain clients but needs to estimate for others based on their background information. Although we might have substantial information about the covariates, the outcome is not known for all individuals. In these cases, supervised learning methods may face limitations, while unsupervised model cannot perform the predictions we want. When the labeled observations are scarce, the supervised model built would bias towards these complete cases, potentially resulting in unsatisfactory performance. Thus, researchers come up with the idea of semi-supervised learning (SSL) that uses both labeled and unlabeled data.

What is SSL?

As its name suggested, SSL is a combination of supervised learning and unsupervised learning, and it is usually used in classification problem. The general form of the objective function is written as a combination as well: \[ \min J=\min (\beta J_{L}+\lambda J_{U}),\, \beta, \lambda \in [0,1]\] Here \(J\) is the overall loss, and \(J_L\) and \(J_U\) represents the loss function based on labeled set and features of all data. \(\beta\) and \(\lambda\) are the regularization parameter to control the effect of two loss function. If \(\beta\) = 1 and \(\lambda\) = 0, this becomes a supervised learning algorithm; if \(\beta\) = 0 and \(\lambda\) = 1, this becomes an unsupervised learning algorithm. Usually, \(\beta\) = 1 is used. (IEEE, bladder cancer)

Self-Training

One of the most easily interpretable semi-supervised learning algorithm is self-training. It first trains a model on the labeled data and generates pseudo labels for the unlabeled data. We choose the most confident unlabeled points with their predicted labels into the training process, and we repeat this process until meeting a stopping criteria. For a dichotomous outcome and the model prediction is the probability being in label 1, these steps can be expressed as:

\[ \begin{aligned} \hat{f}_0 &= \text{arg}\min J_L = \text{arg}\min \frac{1}{N}\sum^N_{i=1}l(\hat{f}_0(x_i),\,y_i) \\ \hat{y_i} &= I \{ \hat{f}_0(x_i) > 0.5 \} \text{ for } x_i \in D_U \\ M &= \sum_{x_i \in D_U} I \{ \max\left(1-\hat{f}_0(x_i) ,\hat{f}_0(x_i)\right) > c \} \\ &\text{ where } c \in (0,1) \text{ is the confidence threshold} \end{aligned} \] We then could have a combination-form objective function to find the optimal \(\hat{f}\) for iteration t:

\[ \begin{aligned} \hat{f}_t &= \text{arg}\min \{J_L + \lambda J_U\} \\ &= \text{arg}\min \{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{x_i \in D_L}l(\hat{f}_{t}(x_i, \, y_i))\\ &+\lambda \frac{1}{M}\sum_{x_j\in D_U}l(\hat{f}_{t}(x_j, \, \hat{y_j})\cdot I[\max\left(1-\hat{f}_{t-1}(x_i) ,\hat{f}_{t-1}(x_i)\right) > c] \} \\ & \text{where } t = 1, ..., T \end{aligned} \]

After finding \(\hat{f}_t\), we then repeat the steps for generating pseudo labels and selecting the most confident ones for the next iteration t+1, and their expressions should be similar to the ones weve shown in the initialization step above. (wisc)

Assumptions of SSL

SSL is a powerful algorithm that utilizes the information from the unlabeled data. However, its performance is based on three strong assumption: smoothness, low-density, manifold. (survey)

We first assume that the data points are close together in the input space should have the same labels. Thus, the change in labels given the input space should be smooth instead of stochastic. We secondly assume that the decision boundary should lie in a region where observed data points are few. In other words, the decision boundary should pass through the low-density region of observed data. This makes sense with the smoothness assumption: if the decision boundary is within a high-density region, many points with similar covariates may have different labels, which is against the smoothness assumption.

Lastly, we assume that data points lying on the same manifold have the same label, where manifold is the lower-dimensional substructures with high-density observed data compared to the high-dimensional input space. This helps us to determine the labels of the unlabeled data if we can determine the manifolds and the data points lie on these manifold.

Overall, these assumption can be generalized to the cluster assumption, and we do see many common points between these assumptions and clustering. For example, data points with similar covariates should be grouped into the same cluster, and we can use this similarity to re-explain our three main assumptions: points are close together are similar; points in high-density area are similar; points lie on the same manifold are similar. Thus, we see how strongly SSL is connected with the unsupervised learning. On the other hand, if we cannot cluster the data in a meaningful way, SSL cannot improve the performance of a supervised learning model. (survey)

What is Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)?

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a class of deep, feed-forward neural networks, most commonly applied in analyzing imaging data, especially for image recognition and classification. They are explicitly designed to process pixel data and can take in an input image, assign importance (learnable weights and biases) to various aspects/objects in the image and be able to differentiate one from the other. The architecture of a CNN is designed to take the key information of the input image. This is achieved with local connections and tied weights followed by some form of pooling which results in translation invariant features. It is easier to train and have many fewer parameters than fully connected vanilla feed-forward neural networks.

The Input Feature

Generally, the raw input image and the features of each hidden layer are represented by a 3D tensor \(\textbf{X} \in R^{d_1 \times d_2 \times d_3}\). Here, the first two dimensions \(d_1, d_2\) of \(\textbf{X}\) represents spatial coordinates of an image while the third dimension \(d_3\) indicates the number of channels. For instance, for the raw inputs, the CT image as grey scale image has \(d_3=1\); if the image has red, green and blue channels, then for the raw inputs, \(d_3=3\). But for the hidden layers, \(d_3\) can be much larger. Additionally, each channel is also called a feature map.

The Architecture of CNNs

There are two key building blocks of CNNs, namely the convolutional layer (linear transforms) and the pooling layer (nonlinear gating). Besides, we also use fully-connected layers as building blocks in the last few layers of a CNN. We will introduce these layers separately as below.

Convolutional Layer

A convolutional layer has the same functionality as the vanilla feed-forward nerual network \(z^{(i)}=\sigma^{(i)}(W^{(i)}z^{(i-1)}+b^{(i)})\), where the input feature \(\textbf{X} \in R^{d_1 \times d_2 \times d_3}\) goes through an affine transformation first and then an element-wise nonlinear activation. But the difference between a convolutional layer and the vanilla feed-forward network hidden layer lies in the specific form of the affine transformation.

A convolutional layer uses numbers of filters to extract local features from the previous input. To be specific, each filter is represented by a 3D tensor \(\textbf{F}_k \in R^{w \times w \times d_3} \quad (1 \leq k \leq \tilde{d}_3)\), where \(w\) is the size of the filter (usually small, e.g. 3, 5) and \(\tilde{d}_3\) denotes the total number of filters. And \(d_3\) here for the third dimension is equal to that of the input feature \(\textbf{X}\). To be brief, one usually says that the filter has size \(w \times w\). Each filter \(\textbf{F}_k\) then convolves with the input feature \(\textbf{X}\) to obtain one single feature map \(\textbf{O}^k \in R^{(d_1-w+1)\times (d_1-w+1)}\), where \[ \textbf{O}^k_{ij}=<[\textbf{X}]_{ij},\textbf{F}_k>=\sum_{i'=1}^{w} \sum_{j'=1}^{w} \sum_{l=1}^{d_3} [\textbf{X}]_{i+i'-1,j+j'-1,l}[\textbf{F}_k]_{i',j',l}, \] which is a locally weighted average of \([\textbf{X}]_{ij}\) using weight \(\textbf{F}_k\). From the expression above, we can see that every average is taken from a small patch of \(\textbf{X}\) starting at location \((i,j)\).

Pooling Layer

A pooling layer aggregates the information of nearby features into a single one. This is a downsampling process, which reduces the size of the features for subsequent layers and save the computation time and memory. To simplify the mathematical expression of pooling layer, we introduce the basic and common form of the pooling layer, which is composed of the \(2\times 2\) max-pooling filter. The \(2\times 2\) max-pooling filter calculates \(\max \{X_{i,j,k},X_{i+1,j,k},X_{i,j+1,k},,X_{i+1,j+1,k}\}\), which is the maximum of the \(2 \times 2\) neighborhood in the spatial coordinates. And the pooling is processed for each feature map \(k\) (channel). A \(2 \times 2\) max-pooling filter will result in an output of size \(d_1/2 \times d_2/2 \times d_3\). Additionally, the pooling layer does not involve any parameters to optimize. It is designed to reduce redundancy because a small neighborhood around a location \((i,j)\) is likely to contain the same information.

An example of Pooling Layer

Fully-connected Layer

A fully-connected layer, also known as a linear layers, is typically placed at the end of the network, after the convolutional and pooling layers. The purpose of the fully-connected layer is to take the high-level features extracted by the preceding layers and perform classification or regression tasks based on those features. Each fully-connected layer has the mathematical form \[ z^{(i)}=\sigma^{(i)}(W^{(i)}z^{(i-1)}+b^{(i)}),\] which is as the same functionality as the vanilla feed-forward neural network.

Appliaction to CT Images

We will run the semi-supervised learning with CNN on a set of CT images. We set our baseline model as the CNN model fitting on the labeled data for comparison. We also fit a CNN model fitting on the whole training set with the true labels. The goal of classification is to identify if the image is scanned with contrast or without contrast.

Data

We are going to analyze a small set of CT medical images data set from a cancer imaging archive with a categorical variable contrast and a continuous variable patient age . The data format is DICOM, and there are 100 files of 2D DICOM. Each DICOM represents the middle slice of the patients CT scan having 512*512 pixel dimensions. A sample with contrast and another without contrast are shown below.

A sample of CT image with contrast

A sample of CT image without contrast

Pre-processing and short exploratory data analysis

For the features: The original 2D scan has \(512 \times 512\) pixel dimensions, which is memory-consuming. We used smoothing average to reduce the resolution of these 2D scans to \(256 \times 256\) pixel dimensions. These 2D scans saved as arrays of gray colors.
For the labels: There are 100 scans in total, with 50 images with contrast and 50 images without contrast. Therefore the data is balanced.
Train-test split for supervised learning: We random selected 20 images (20%) as the test data set with 10 images with the label of with contrast and 10 images with the label of without contrast. The remaining 80 images are treated as the full training data. The training and test data are still balanced.
Drop labels for SSL: We randomly selected 40 images in total for dropping the labels with 20 images with the label of with contrast and 20 images with the label of without contrast. The remaining 40 scans in the training data with labels are still balanced and are treated as the training data for supervised learning with limited labeled images.

Semi-supervised learning

Then, based on the CNN trained on this limited 40 labeled images, we extend the model to SSL by self-training algorithm. We iteratively generates pseudo labels for the unlabeled images and choose the most confident unlabeled images with their predicted labels into the training process, and we repeat this process for ten times. The choice of ten times is to save the running time and reduce the memory consuming here. Additionally, the most confident means the predicted probability is greater than 0.95 or less than 0.05. We evaluate the SSL model on the test data set and the result is shown as below.

We can see that the AUC of SSL is 0.84, which is very close to the supervised learning with only 40 labeled images as training data. However, the sensitivity, specificity, accuracy and also the precision improved after self-training and pseudo labeling process.
